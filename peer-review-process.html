Peer Review Process

The process of peer review involves systematically evaluating articles to assess their validity, quality, and often their originality for publication. It is designed to uphold the integrity of science by screening out articles that are unacceptable or of poor quality. The peer-review process in AJRST consists of six steps.

  1. Author— The research work is submitted to AJRST by authors either through online submission or by Email. Authors have the option to submit their research paper in either AJRST manuscript word format or plain word format. If authors face challenges in formatting the manuscript according to AJRST guidelines, they can submit it in normal word format, and our associate editors will convert it to the standard AJRST format.

  2. Editor— Received manuscripts are provided with unique IDs by the Editor-in-Chief (EIC). Each article undergoes plagiarism, grammatical, and formatting checks. To proceed to the next stage, the plagiarism level must not exceed 30%. Should a manuscript fail to meet these criteria, it will be sent back to the corresponding author for revisions. Manuscripts that meet all requirements are assigned to a Reviewer with relevant expertise, based on the manuscript's abstract and keywords. The assigned Reviewer manages the peer review process. The journal employs a double-blind review process, with each paper being reviewed by at least two reviewers.

  3. Reviewer— Two reviewers, who are subject experts, will assess your manuscripts based on their relevance and the quality of the content, and assign a score to each manuscript, which is out of 100. The reviewers evaluating a paper are not aware of each other. The scorecards from these two independent reviewers are then submitted to the Editor-in-Chief.

  4. Editor— The EIC will calculate the average of the ratings provided by the two reviewers. This composite score is essential for progressing to the next phase and for a chance to be featured in the best paper section (special issue) of the month, where authors are recognized. Based on the scores, the EIC may either accept the paper as is or with modifications, or reject it. The author will be notified of the peer-review outcome and provided with the manuscript ID via Email from the editor.

  5. Author— Upon acceptance of the manuscript, the author is required to complete the final procedures, such as submitting a duly signed copyright form and a screenshot of the payment proof.

  6. Editor— Once the aforementioned process is completed, the editor will submit the paper for publication in the current issue. Upon successful publication, the editor will notify the corresponding author via Email. The author will receive a PDF copy of the published paper and individual E-certificates for all the authors mentioned in the paper. At the end of the month, the EIC will select the best paper(s) based on the scores obtained for all the manuscripts reviewed during the peer-review process for that month.

The main parameters used to calculate the paper marks (out of 100) for the evaluation of each type of submissions are:

  • Importance (45 pts): A manuscript's importance lies in its relevance to readers and its impact on various aspects such as social life, the economy, and the scientific community. Topics that address current societal issues, offer solutions to economic challenges, or advance scientific understanding typically score higher. The ability to contribute meaningfully to these areas determines the manuscript's importance.

  • Degree of Plagiarism (10 pts): The degree of plagiarism directly affects the manuscript's originality and scholarly integrity. Manuscripts with minimal plagiarism (≤ 5%) uphold high academic standards and contribute new insights. Higher levels of plagiarism (≤ 15% or ≤ 30%) result in lower scores, reflecting reliance on existing work without sufficient attribution or innovation.
    • Plagiarism ≤ 5% ⇒ 10 pts
    • Plagiarism ≤ 15% ⇒ 5 pts
    • Plagiarism ≤ 30% ⇒ 3 pts

  • Content (45 pts): Content quality includes clarity, logical coherence, language proficiency, thoroughness, and layout. Clear expression, logical arguments, correct grammar, and thorough coverage of research topics enhance scholarly impact and accessibility. An organized layout facilitates efficient information access. These aspects collectively determine the manuscript's academic rigor and contribution.
